The midterm project is a chance for you to give an ugly but popular website a fresh coat of paint using HTML & CSS!

Overview

Your task is to redesign and code the website for King Pin Donuts, either on your own or with a partner. If you are working alone, it should have at least two distinct "views" -- we require Home and Menu pages. If you are working with a partner, we require a third distinct "view" -- either Our Story or Find Us. Everything else is up to you!

This means that you have the creative freedom to add or remove content, change up the layouts to your liking, and customize the visual design. Read this whole page for complete details on grading, deadlines, and deliverables.

Reminder: While coding up your website, it is essential to link multiple pages properly so the audience can easily navigate the website by following links on your webpages. If you're unsure how to, ask us!

Why King Pin Donuts?

King Pin Donuts suffers from a design problem, some of which we have explored in class -- lack of visual hierarchy, poor typography, improper spacing, color usage, etc. If this website is meant to attract a customer, it’s not doing a very good job of it, even if the donuts themselves are delicious. We want to be able to redesign this website such that its appearance is able to attract new and consistent customers onsite.

More details about each component is explained below.

Project Check-in (4 points)

You are required to check-in with your progress with WDD staff at their office hours before class on Thursday, October 19th. We have office hours every week, so please try to complete this early so you won't be waiting in line for other students on the last day.

To get full credit for this part, you must come with: completed mockups (home + menu pages, one additional page if working with a partner) of your site created in Figma (or whatever tool you're used to) in high-fidelity. We will review your design decisions and offer design/programming suggestions.

Visual Design (10 points)

Here are some things to consider as you design your site.

  • Color scheme: Are colors too bright or too dark? Is there enough contrast between the text color and background color?
  • Typography: Is the font readable? Are different pieces of text appropriately sized?
  • Visual hierarchy/Layout: Is content organized logically? Is there sufficient spacing?
  • Overall: Do your visual elements convey the mood you want to create on the site? Do your design choices outline visual hierarchy? Show your friends/roommates/classmates - what do they think?

User Experience (10 points)

For UX, think broad, about purpose. Here are some user experience considerations to think about

  • Does the user have a consistent experience across the whole site?
  • Is navigation clear and accessible?
  • Is content (menus, location, contact info, etc) easy to find?
  • Does this website make me want to go eat here?

Note on Functionality: We understand that we haven’t covered things like how to add a Google Maps widget, functioning buttons, or animations to your site. For more advanced functionality like that, we don’t expect a full implementation. For example, an image of a map would be sufficient.

Write-up (4 points)

You are also responsible for a write-up explaining the design decisions you made throughout this project. Your design decisions should revolve around the key concepts discussed in class, including spacing, typography, and color. Aim for this document to be around 200-250 words. Be concise about your design choices!

Attach your completed write-up in your Google Form submission.

Note on Images: You can feel free to use any images you think would be appropriate for a donut shop website. However, if you use images that you found on the internet, you must cite your sources (a link to the website you found it from is fine) at the end of your write-up.

A great site we recommend for finding stock photos is unsplash.com.

Recommended Workflow

The general steps that we recommend to complete this project are as follows:

  1. Look at the King Pin Donuts website. What do they do well and what can be improved? Decide on the content of each page.
  2. Create the general page structure with a wireframe. Draw by hand! Re-iterate and decide what works before moving on.
  3. Mock up both pages, including content and images in Figma. Play with the color scheme and fonts and decide on a consistent theme. As you do this, think about the design decisions you’re making, which you’ll need to talk about in the write-up.
  4. Attend any TA or instructor’s office hours for your project check-in.
  5. Finalize the design, incorporating the TA’s feedback. This is also a good time to start the write-up.
  6. Create the HTML skeletons and CSS that would be used across every page - for example, the navigation links would have consistent CSS on every page of the site.
  7. Now that you have a “base,” you can more easily finish up the remaining pages.
  8. Check that the whole site has a cohesive look and feel.
  9. Complete the write-up and submit!
